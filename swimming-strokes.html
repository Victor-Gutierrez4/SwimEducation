Swimming Strokes

Learn the four main swimming strokes: Freestyle, Backstroke, Breaststroke, and Butterfly. These strokes are the foundation of competitive swimming and water fitness.

Freestyle

Freestyle, also known as front crawl, is the fastest and most efficient stroke. It's commonly used in races like the 100m and 200m freestyle.

  • Body Position: Horizontal, streamlined position with face in the water.
  • Arm Movement: Alternating arm strokes, one arm pulling while the other recovers above water.
  • Leg Movement: Flutter kick, legs alternate in an up-and-down motion.
  • Breathing: Turn your head to the side to inhale, exhale through your nose underwater.

Backstroke

Backstroke is the only stroke swum on the back. It is a relaxing, yet effective stroke for building endurance and strength.

  • Body Position: Floating on your back, keeping your body straight and flat.
  • Arm Movement: Alternating arm strokes, palms facing outward during the pull phase.
  • Leg Movement: Flutter kick, similar to freestyle but with your face above the water.
  • Breathing: Natural breathing while floating on your back.

Breaststroke

The breaststroke is a slower stroke but it is highly efficient and easy on the body. It’s great for beginners and long-distance swimmers.

  • Body Position: Horizontal with the head above water for breathing.
  • Arm Movement: Symmetrical arm strokes, pulling with both arms at the same time in a half-circle motion.
  • Leg Movement: Frog kick, legs perform a circular motion, bringing knees towards the chest before pushing outward.
  • Breathing: Breathe during the arm recovery phase, with the head lifted above water.

Butterfly

The butterfly stroke is one of the most challenging yet rewarding strokes, known for its power and speed.

  • Body Position: Undulating movement with the body moving like a wave.
  • Arm Movement: Both arms move simultaneously in a circular motion above and below the water.
  • Leg Movement: Dolphin kick, where both legs move together in a fluid up-and-down motion.
  • Breathing: Head comes out of the water during the arm recovery phase to inhale, exhale underwater.
